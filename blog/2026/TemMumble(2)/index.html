2] Coupling

2.1] Definition

From joint distribution to coupling.

Note taht this set is alwasy nonempty, because we could always take the product measure (\(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, if you may)

2.2] Examples

2.2.1] Deterministic vs. Product Coupling

Almost the opposite of product coupling and they are almost never the same, even in your setting where

\[\nu = \Phi_{\#}\mu .\]

Let’s go through the structure carefully, first of all deterministic coupling induced by the map. Let

\[X \sim \mu, \qquad Y = \Phi(X).\]

Then the joint distribution of \((X,Y)\) is

\[\gamma = (\text{Id},\phi)_{\#}\mu\]

Explicitly, for measurable sets \(A,B\):

\[\gamma(A \times B) = \mu\big(\{x\in A : \phi(x)\in B\}\big).\]

This coupling has marginals

  • first marginal: \(\mu\)
  • second marginal: \(\phi_{\#}\mu = \nu\)

so it is indeed a coupling between \(\mu\) and \(\nu\).

The key property for the above joint law is that the conditional distribution satisfies

\[Y|X=x \sim \delta_{\phi(x)}(y).\]

So \(Y\) is completely determined by \(X\). This is why it is called a deterministic coupling.

On the other hand, there always exist the produc coupling:

\[\gamma_{\text{prod}} = \mu \otimes \nu.\]

For measurable sets:

\[\gamma_{\text{prod}}(A \times B) = \mu(A)\nu(B).\]

Its conditional distribution is

\[Y|X=x \sim \nu.\]

So here

  • \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.

The deterministic coupling and the product coupling are different. The deterministic coupling lives on the graph of \(\phi\):

\[\{(x,\phi(x))\}.\]

whereas the product coupling spreads mass over the entire product space.

Well, this actually has some practical advantages for transport map instead of coupling (transport plan) in terms of parameter storage, and in fact quite often we will be able to find a transport map in optimal transport problem, which is indeed in most cases preferrable. We will come back to this later.

2.2.2] An Example

Let

\[X \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1], \qquad \Phi(x)=x.\]

Then

\[\nu = \mu.\]

For the deterministic coupling, the joint law is

\[(X,X)\]

Support: \(\{(x,x)\}\), which is just the diagonal line.

On the other hand, for the product coupling, the joint law stays on

\[(X,Y), \quad X,Y \text{ i.i.d. Uniform}[0,1]\]

where the support is \([0,1]^2\), which is the whole square.

These are clearly different distributions.

2.2.3] When Would They Coincide?

They coincide only in degenerate cases. For example if \(\nu\) is a Dirac measure

\[\nu = \delta_{y_0}\]

and

\[\phi(x)=y_0.\]

Then

  • deterministic coupling: $(X,y_0)$
  • product coupling: $(X,Y)$ with $Y=y_0$ a.s.

So they match.

But this is exceptional.

5. Key conceptual point

Knowing that

\[\nu = \phi_{\#}\mu\]

does not restrict couplings to deterministic ones.

It only tells you one particular coupling exists:

\[(\text{Id},\phi)_{\#}\mu .\]

But the set of couplings

\[\Pi(\mu,\nu)\]

is huge — it includes

  • deterministic couplings
  • product coupling
  • infinitely many others.

6. Transport perspective

In optimal transport, the deterministic coupling

\[(\text{Id},\phi)_{\#}\mu\]

is called a transport map.

But most couplings are transport plans that cannot be written as maps.

✅ Summary

  • Deterministic coupling:

    \[\gamma = (\text{Id},\phi)_{\#}\mu\]

    $Y=\phi(X)$

  • Product coupling:

    \[\gamma = \mu\otimes\nu\]

    $X$ and $Y$ independent.

They are almost never the same.

If you’d like, I can also explain a geometric picture of all couplings when $\nu=\phi_{#}\mu$ (it clarifies how deterministic couplings sit inside the full coupling polytope and why optimal transport often selects them).

3】 Clarifications and Differences

It’s not always possible to establish a transport map from \(\mu\) to \(\nu\).

For example, the Kronecker Delta.

However, apart from this, in general is there a transport map?

If we could then find out a transport map between \(\mu\) to \(\nu\), then does that mean that there exists a deterministic coupling among others?
