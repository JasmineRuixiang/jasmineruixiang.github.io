4.3] Another Interpretation of Conditional Expectation

The statement you’re referring to is the Hilbert space interpretation of conditional expectation. The idea is that conditional expectation is the best (L^2) approximation (least-squares projection) of a random variable onto the space of functions measurable with respect to the conditioning σ-algebra.

I’ll explain the pieces carefully.

1. The (L^2) space of random variables

Consider a probability space ((\Omega,\mathcal F,P)).

Define

\[L^2(\Omega) = \{X : \mathbb{E}[X^2] < \infty \}.\]

This is a Hilbert space with inner product

\[\langle X,Y \rangle = \mathbb{E}[XY].\]

The norm is

\[\|X\|_2 = \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]}.\]

This is exactly the mean-square norm.

2. The subspace of measurable functions

Let (\mathcal G \subseteq \mathcal F) be a σ-algebra.

Define

\[L^2(\mathcal G)\]

as the set of all square-integrable random variables measurable with respect to (\mathcal G).

This is a closed linear subspace of (L^2(\Omega)).

So geometrically:

  • (L^2(\Omega)) = a Hilbert space
  • (L^2(\mathcal G)) = a subspace

3. Conditional expectation as orthogonal projection

For any (X \in L^2(\Omega)), the conditional expectation

\[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal G]\]

is the orthogonal projection of (X) onto (L^2(\mathcal G)).

Meaning:

\[\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G] = \operatorname*{argmin}_{Y \in L^2(\mathcal G)} \mathbb{E}[(X-Y)^2]\]

So it minimizes the (L^2) error.

4. Meaning of “(L^2) normalization error”

The phrase you saw likely meant:

Conditional expectation minimizes the (L^2) error (mean-square error) among all (\mathcal G)-measurable functions.

Explicitly,

\[\mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G])^2] = \min_{Y \in L^2(\mathcal G)} \mathbb{E}[(X-Y)^2]\]

So if you want to approximate (X) using only information in (\mathcal G), the best approximation in mean-square sense is the conditional expectation.

5. Orthogonality condition

The projection property implies

\[\mathbb{E}\big[(X-\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G])Y\big] = 0 \quad \forall Y \in L^2(\mathcal G)\]

So the error is orthogonal to the subspace.

This is the Hilbert-space analogue of least squares regression.

6. Simple example

Suppose

  • (X) = tomorrow’s temperature
  • (\mathcal G) = today’s weather information

Then

\[\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G]\]

is the best mean-square prediction of tomorrow’s temperature using today’s information.

No other (\mathcal G)-measurable predictor has smaller

\[\mathbb{E}[(X-Y)^2]\]

7. Geometric picture

Think of:

  • (X) = a vector in Hilbert space
  • (L^2(\mathcal G)) = a subspace

Then

\[\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G]\]

is the perpendicular projection of (X) onto that subspace.

And

\[X = \mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G] + (X-\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G])\]

with the two parts orthogonal.

8. Why this interpretation is powerful

This viewpoint connects probability with:

  • Hilbert space theory
  • least squares regression
  • martingales
  • signal filtering (Kalman filters)

For example, linear regression is just a finite-dimensional case of this projection principle.

✅ In one sentence

Conditional expectation is the orthogonal projection of a random variable onto the subspace of functions measurable with respect to the conditioning σ-algebra, which means it minimizes the (L^2) (mean-square) approximation error.
