3] An Important Lemma

3.1]

Why doest \(\Phi_t\) being a diffeomorphism lead to the fact that \(\det(\nabla \Phi_t) > 0\)?

Well, the key point is that being a diffeomorphism forces the Jacobian determinant to never vanish, but the positivity comes from continuity in time together with the initial condition. Let’s go step by step.

In the Lagrangian view of optimal transport we have a flow map

\[\partial_t \Phi_t(x) = V_t(\Phi_t(x)), \qquad \Phi_0(x) = x.\]

Here

  • \(\Phi_t : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d\) is the flow map
  • \(V_t\) is a smooth velocity field.

So \(\Phi_t\) is generated by an ODE flow.

Then, if \(\Phi_t\) is a diffeomorphism, then

  • \(\Phi_t\) is smooth
  • \(\Phi_t^{-1}\) exists and is smooth.

By the inverse function theorem, this implies

\[\det(\nabla \Phi_t(x)) \neq 0\]

for every \(x\). Otherwise the map would locally collapse dimension and would not be invertible. So diffeomorphism naturally leads to Jacobian determinant never vanishes.

Then Why is the determinant is positive? Now consider the initial condition \(\Phi_0(x) = x\), so

\[\nabla \Phi_0(x) = I.\]

Thus

\[\det(\nabla \Phi_0(x)) = 1.\]

An important fact: The determinant evolves continuously in time because \(\Phi_t\) is smooth in \(t\)! If the determinant were ever to become negative, it would have to cross zero at some time. But

\[\det(\nabla \Phi_t(x)) = 0\]

would violate the diffeomorphism property. Therefore the determinant cannot change sign. Since it starts positive,

\[\det(\nabla \Phi_t(x)) > 0\]

for all \(t\).

3.2] Geometric interpretation

\(\det(\nabla \Phi_t)\) measures local volume distortion.

  • positive determinant → orientation preserved
  • negative determinant → orientation flipped

A smooth flow starting from the identity cannot suddenly flip orientation without collapsing volume first. But collapsing volume would break invertibility. So the flow preserves orientation.

3.3] Another way to see it (Jacobian ODE)

Define

\[J_t(x) = \det(\nabla \Phi_t(x)).\]

Then one can derive

\[\frac{d}{dt} J_t(x) = (\nabla \cdot V_t)(\Phi_t(x))\, J_t(x).\]

Solving:

\[J_t(x) = \exp\!\left( \int_0^t (\nabla\cdot V_s)(\Phi_s(x))\,ds \right).\]

Since the exponential is always positive,

\[J_t(x) > 0.\]

3.3] Final intuition

A diffeomorphic flow generated by a smooth velocity field:

  • starts from the identity
  • continuously deforms space
  • never tears or folds space

Therefore

\[\det(\nabla \Phi_t) > 0\]

because the map cannot cross zero Jacobian without losing invertibility.
