The King of Lockpickers

 

It was a normal rainy day, and I was again locked outside my apartment. Always been forgetful. I turned around, walked a few steps until I stood in front of a wall. Every day, as I walked out or came home, I always saw that same wall and always felt heavy as though my body had been turned into rusty steel. It was initially white, as you could tell, though after several decades with no one cleaning it became irrecoverably grey and dirty. If there was anything new or showcased the passage of time, it would be the phone numbers lockpickers put on.

 

I lived in a small and developing city, and this department was first established in 1990s. No elevators, no managers, and no fancy coffee machine or billiards table. I was almost an exception here because most residents were the elder people. Sometimes when I climbed upstairs walking past them, I would intentionally slow down my pace and tried not to have them surpassed as they managed hardly to reach their level. Life was exhibiting its cruel truth as aging was always being so faithful to time, constantly reminding people that what they cherished and were proud of in youth were so conveniently either demolished or snatched away in later years. In rainy days at night the lights in the corridors at each floor would get faint and always alternated between on and off, so looking from outside it seemed like whole building was vitally twinkling its eyes. At such times I would always stand at the entrance, feeling doubly poignant since the degrading building and its aging residents were so discordant to such vibration of light. Looking upwards at each window, I felt helpless as I was aware many would be haunted by arthritis and rheumatism the whole night not able to sleep.

 

The rain got tight and heavy. I looked at the wall and felt encompassed by the numbers. It’s hard to count exactly how many lockpickers put their names here. Each of them looked drastically similar: always with their name in the format of “King of Lock Pickers” added with their family name and the phone number below. Every lockpicker claimed himself/herself the king, and since each would use different colors, the entire wall appeared as a huge punk graffiti, except the doodlers would almost know nothing of punk. Each lockpicker managed to make themselves stand out from the rest and they adopted different strategies. Some printed locally their names and numbers repeatedly for tens of twenties times, so people would immediately recognize it from the rest disarray of numbers. Some would spread out their tags sporadically to maximize the chance of randomly finding them. It was not surprising to see that someone print their tags above others, and the entire wall exhibited the most blatant signal of the ground level harsh competition. I never knew when any new lockpicker came to print their names but every morning I walked past the wall, I caught some new names covering the old. When I lied on my cozy bed at time imagining some lockpickers would spend their whole night printing their tags on virtually every wall in the building, I felt so sorry and shuddered.

 

I randomly picked a “king” since he shared the same surname with me. Within 10 minutes a young man appeared in my view. He looked roughly at my age, with dark curly hair and dressed in jackets faded in blue. His glasses were a little at odds with my usual impression of a lockpicker. His skinny body looked drastically frail compared with the huge took kit he took on his back. When he walked upstairs and arrived my door, he panted heavily. I could tell his face looked pale.

 

It was raining more heavily outside and lightning occasionally flashed. He started working after we briefly greeted each other. He was slightly trembling as I looked into his eyes, and I was a little worried whether his technique was reliable. “Is there anything wrong?” “No, it went smoothly.” His voice was dry reminding me of shriveled wheat. As he managed to insert a wire inside the keyhole, I looked outside wondering when the rain would stop.

 

I heard a clear cut. I turned around and as I was about to thank him for his help, the stiff expression on his face made me swallow my words. He was holding a broken wire at hand, and I saw his face gradually twisted until he let out a shriek that made me shudder.

 

I didn’t know what to say. He looked at me as if wanting to pierce me through though his eyes were filled with despair and terror.  “No, no, no…” he kept muttering and swiftly turning himself around, as if trying to hold something tight and solid. “Hey, it’s ok. It’s ok.” I awkwardly commented. “I’m sorry. I’m sorry. No…” “Hey everything is fine. I won’t blame you. This is…” “No, not this.” I understood how this occasion might be the straw that would eventually break him apart, whatever he had experienced. People were fragile. No matter how solid and resilient they appeared, they would always have a soft spot that time and destiny would hopelessly find and poke through at last. The old gentle lady living upstairs passed away in stroke peacefully at dream last week, and I remembered when we chatted, she appeared normal and talkative the afternoon on the day of her death. I was not particularly worrying severe conditions for the lockpicker, but that I thought I should offer him care and warmness in such a depressing rainy day. “Then tell me. Everything is going to be alright. Let’s sit here and take some rest.” I dragged him away from the lock and set us on the staircase. He suspiciously looked at me, perplexed yet he gradually stopped shuddering and after minutes of silence he started speaking.

 

“I was reluctant to see her suffer.”

“Who?”

“My grandma. I felt so much in despair.”

 

By putting together his scattered narrations I eventually developed a clear picture of the situation. His old grandma recently became a Christian and decided to accept his grandpa. The story should be extended to two decades ago. Grandpa once betrayed Grandma and they lived separately ever since. The entire family still got weakly connected as his mom would transfer money monthly to Grandpa, though in most senses Grandpa was literally separated from the family. Yet starting from last year, Grandpa got worse on his health situations and was hospitalized several times. It turned out it’s not safe for Grandpa to live himself. Then Grandma decided to welcome Grandpa back to take care of him.

 

“Why did your grandma make that decision? It must be a hard one.”

“Christianity.”

 

The lockpicker spoke the word with controlled rage, “it was all Christianity. I don’t understand. She said after she became a Christian, she knew that the lord required her to be permissive and show mercy. I still remember when I talked with her about my incomprehension and doubt, she replied with light laughter and quoted from some of her Christian script that when someone got slapped in the left face, he/she should turn the right face to be slapped again. But you know, the question is, she was not really laughing!”

 

He took a stop. I saw tears welling up in his eyes and encouraged him to continue. “Thank you for listening. I was really about to collapse. I loved her so much and I couldn’t withstand seeing her trapped in the Christian belief and weaved lies for herself. She was not really laughing. I saw a slice of bitter in her smile. I wanted the answer at that time, you know, though now recalling back I realized I might be too aggressive. At that time, I asked her if she was trying to paralyze herself and whether she indeed accepted Grandpa from heart.”

 

Dear lockpicker, you were being so considerate and tentative. One of the core differences between philosophy and religion was that while the former strived to actively uncover truth via the path of doubt, the latter required passive reception of certain “truth”, or in some sense ignorance. I understood that your grandma didn’t sink into the abysmal concept of hinter-worldly life, which degraded the meaning of any earthly life that could have been so vibrantly achieved. However, in your context Christianity was appearing in another face, which didn’t sound with affected solemness and thus not trustable, but in the appearance as a merciful guider that patted her shoulder and said you were permitted live on as long as you believed in me.

 

I didn’t voice my opinion to the lockpicker because he continued: “You know how she replied? She was silent for a second and she spoke, word by word, slowly and clearly, that she wanted peace at heart.”

 

We were silent. I could see that he felt remorse for having forced answers from his grandma, which sounded cruel indeed even for me. Clearly, he didn’t believe his grandma would forgive Grandpa, and, more importantly, he himself didn’t forgive Grandpa for betraying Grandma. Yet I was still unclear what kind of peace his grandma intended to achieve. Did she feel pity for Grandpa’s worsening state of health? Did it come out of sympathy/empathy from being the elderly? Or was it that old people intended to forget about the ugly sorrowful past and chose to have some restful decades without being haunted by the memory? I could never know the exact answer. The lockpicker didn’t continue. I knew sometimes knowing half truth was reassuringly enough.

 

“To be honest, I was not persuaded. That’s not peace, but only lies. She didn’t completely will to accept Grandpa. I could tell. You might not know, my grandma was a respectable person, and maybe she would consider Grandpa’s betrayal a serious failure of her life. However she thought, I didn’t ask more. I saw her tears running down and I began to hate myself for being too pushy, even in the name for her good. Eh, anyway, whatever the true reasons might be, it’s not that important. Digging out the truth, for both her and me, might just be unimportant. The key is, to live on. That’s our philosophy, right?” He gave me a bitter smile, “But, sorry to keep you here, but occasionally I would feel I was not doing enough for her. I wanted to help her go one step deeper into her mind, so that her idea would be clearly revealed. Shouldn’t we try to see ourselves clearly at all costs?” He seemed to feel he was elaborating on this topic for too long, and swiftly changed the subject: “Eh, right now just as I walked upstairs, I saw an old man standing midway on the staircase and didn’t move. I went to him, and he told me he needed some rest after climbing those several steps. My heart bumped astoundingly fast, and I felt like couldn’t breathe. Eventually I carried him home on my pack and when I arrived here, I must look so terrible. I was haunted by the idea that my grandma was entangled with her belief and was not really happy, yet I didn’t try my best to help her. And later when my tool broke, I also broke…Sorry for everything that happened today, I…”

 

“I’m glad you feel better now. I, I actually have the same idea as yours. But sometimes it costs heavily to see the truth. I’m afraid in your case Christianity carries more positive meaning that the belief itself is constructing another truth for your grandma to live in. Under extreme sorrows, conceptualizing reality from any method should be considered valid. I won’t call it lies. If we were stiffly stuck on the concept of truth and pursuit of complete self-dissection, we might find the world completely shattered and chaotic. We might drive our families shattered and chaotic”

 

The lockpicker nodded in silence. Relentlessly, the rain at the outside was still pouring and I felt that my perception of time was becoming heavy and sticky. “I’m a graduate student and now it’s vacation so I went out to take some work. In college I read philosophy, but it turned out it was much difficult to apply it in real life, whether Kantian or Nietzschean, especially for people you care. You easily became too analytical and impassive. Well, I guess for my grandma, there’re other reasons for her choice. You might know that people from her age tended to be overly thrifty.”

 

“The old people tend to be thrifty.”

“True. It’s difficult for them to change or even correct their habits which have been established for decades. But that’s not my point here. The 1960s, when my grandma was young, that was an age when most families were in severe lack of “substances”.”

 

I immediately understood what he meant. During those 20 years when the government was managing to reconstruct the whole nation, and before the waves of necessary economic and social reforms, the whole society was lacking in most of substances. Most families didn’t even see electricity and it would be lucky to have meat within a week. It turned out the lockpicker’s Grandma was from that generation.

 

“They had to be frugal every day. That was quite a difficult time. But things changed now, and we are all in an abundant world. However, the harsh fact is, the world and the country changed but my grandma couldn’t follow. She still finished every granule of rice at dining table and would wear the same coat for more than 9 years even if it heavily worn out. I remembered once when I finished the yoghurt, I tightened the cap and threw the bottle into the trash bin. I usually won’t drink the yoghurt on the inside of the cap. The next day I found the bottle and the cap separately placed on the dining table, and there’s no yoghurt on the cap. My grandma was sitting there looking at me intently…” The lockpicker’s voice was trembling, and he turned to me intently, “you know then afterwards I would lick away the yoghurt on the cap and threw away the bottle and cap separately… That’s little off topic, but I want to say even for simple things that we suppose would be easy to change, it’s difficult for her to walk out of her habits.”

 

“So to reevaluate everything that has been restraining her ever since, including the phantom old habits from the past, is difficult?”

“It is. You know, it’s not that simple as to move away a brick from a wall or cut the fingernails. Memory, like a toy thing time experimented its magic upon, would constantly be altered and filtered. What appeared, no matter beautiful or sorrowful, became the appearance itself for which we were expecting to see reoccurring in the future. It’s like everyone was treated with a bad joke, that in order to live on with assurance, we have to constantly project the past and the memory, as the veil of Maya, into the future, the abundance and infinite probability of which were continuously shrunk and dwindled. Overcoming oneself is difficult.”

 

“Not only difficult, but sometimes we need to even question whether “overcoming” would be so demolishing, especially for people who have been defeated by sorrow and entangled with memory from the past. More importantly, the idea of striving to define a “self”, to cherish such a first-person, subjective perspective[1], might be exactly what she intended to forget. Because the memory and sorrow that came with the pursuit is unbearable. This is the opposite of existentialism. For your grandma, hope that by taking belief in Christianity she would conceptualize and thus reconstruct her own world and truth. Perhaps in this sense overcoming herself is the effort to fully believe in Christianity.”

 

The lock picker’s phone rang, and I stopped my narration abruptly. But my mind was spinning fast. I was reminded of how drunkenness, or being in some sense ignorant, was so hard and yet so valuable. Life rested upon errors and illusions, but for normal people these abstract ideas might be crystalized as substantial sorrows that they felt deeply. And that’s partially why being fluent and dynamic was so crucial as to catch up along with the flow of time. I felt how lonely we actually were, at present age especially, that Kafka’s concept of “the indestructible”[2] was significantly inapplicable since not all people would suffer the same extent, and there’s no deep connection (would people even want to establish such connection?) among all humans. But again, if one could be persuaded to believe in such concept, then there is not much difference between Kafka’s idea and Christianity.

 

“I’m so sorry I must go now. Another client is waiting for me. Could you please contact another lockpicker?”

“No worries. I will just dial another number.” I smiled at him.

“Thank you so much for listening to me. Feeling much better now.”

 

I accompanied him out the building and he departed. It was already evening. Watching him running swiftly into darkness and rain, I didn’t have a chance to hug him or communicated my wish that there be philosophy for people that were overcome and not the overcoming. Who knew how to correctly judge the choice by the lockpicker’s Grandma? Or was I qualified to do so? Was philosophy qualified? Sorrow, time, and memory, so relentless, changed so many concepts when being applied to the people that had been entangled with them the most. Christianity, the last peaceful place of belonging for that grandma, was somehow a sanctum, though built on submission to sorrows, to provide comfort. Viewed from the perspective of life, wasn’t this another sort of art that made it possible that the meaning of living, or simply, living itself, could be carried along with a relative peaceful mind given the unbearable past?

 

I didn’t bring an umbrella and enjoyed being drenched by the rain. I stood at the entrance, looking upward only to find the entire building twinkling its numerous eyes. Suddenly I felt a sense of passion, hoping that all old people in this building could find a sanctum, not necessarily in religious sense, which could afford comfort during the heavy rain. Some humans were bridges, but some were later transformed, whether actively or passively, into various kinds of purposes. To live on with the purposes likewise needed overcoming and strenuous effort to arrive at peace. For those people, it might be equivalently ruthless to require the will to power and to master themselves.

 

I walked upstairs and now I was again in front of the wall with names of different “kings” of lockpickers. The light in the corridor of my level was twinkling slowly, but with a random frequency as if to match with the sounds of rain engulfing me all around. Under the twinkles, the tags on the wall appeared melted and transformed, spreading away with no clear pattern, and turned into different human faces and figures, bowing, and tentatively focusing on cracking open the locks. A spasm flushed through my back as I suddenly realized that everyone was a lockpicker, with our names printed onto the world, waiting to be successfully picked up and to shoot for success and the ultimate meaning of life. But most would be left unattended just as most numbers on the wall would never be dialed once, and even for the luckily picked lockpickers, they might have themselves broken inside the lock while managing to open it. But life continued and there was and always would be a lock to pierce through.

 

The rain was heavy, and I was still locked outside.
