## 1.2] Measure Preservation 

Instead of directly starting with measure preservation, I'd like to begin with a summary of the following important theorem, from which we will unpack all that would follow. 

### 1.2.1] Liouville's Theorem
Indeed, when first encountering Liouville’s theorem, it is easy to get confused by several ideas being mixed together:

- divergence
- flows of vector fields
- volume preservation
- measures
- pushforward measures

The goal of this sub-section is to unpack these concepts carefully and connect them together geometrically.

The big picture goes like this: suppose we have a dynamical system

$$
\frac{dx_t}{dt} = V_t(x_t),
$$

where $$V$$ is a vector field on $$\mathbb{R}^n$$. This vector field generates a flow

$$
\phi_t : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n,
$$

which tells us where each point moves after time $$t$$. The central question we are asking here is:

> Does the flow preserve volume?

and Liouville’s theorem says:

> A flow preserves ordinary volume exactly when the vector field has zero divergence.

More precisely,

$$
\operatorname{div} V_t = 0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
(\phi_t)_\# \lambda = \lambda,
$$

where $$\lambda$$ is the Lebesgue measure (ordinary volume).

To understand this statement, we first need to understand what measures and pushforwards actually are.

---

### 1.2.2] What Is a Measure?
If you have taken measure theory or advanced probabilty theory, you definitely have already learned the concept of measure. The central idea is not complicated, yet there hides a subtle shift of views from the space to its mass, or more precisely, to separate these two as overlapping yet fundamentally distinct entities. 

A `measure` is simply a systematic way to assign a “size” to sets. For examples. 

- length on $$\mathbb{R}$$
- area on $$\mathbb{R}^2$$
- volume on $$\mathbb{R}^3$$
- probability mass in probability theory (indeed, we will refer to these as probability measures)

Formally, a measure $$\mu$$ assigns a nonnegative number to subsets:

$$
A \mapsto \mu(A).
$$

So a measure is not a function on points. A measure takes in subsets and answers the question: “How large is this set?"

---

### 1.2.3] What Is Lebesgue Measure?
In nutshell, Lebesgue measure is the standard notion of volume in Euclidean space. For example, 

- $\mathbb{R}$ → length
- $\mathbb{R}^2$ → area
- $\mathbb{R}^3$ → volume
- $\mathbb{R}^n$ → $n$-dimensional volume

To be less concrete, for an interval,

$$
\lambda([a,b]) = b-a.
$$

For a rectangle,

$$
\lambda([a,b]\times[c,d]) =
(b-a)(d-c).
$$

Thus when people say:

> “the flow preserves Lebesgue measure”

they simply mean:

> “the flow preserves ordinary volume.”

Notice that here already we have released ourselves from the classical/assumed *space = volume* narrative. Here a space is just a collection or a set, without any other predefined structures. Of course we could make it more precise, like a metric space ($$(X, d)$$) or a topological space ($$(X, O_X \subset P_X)$$) but fundamentally their components are just the set $$X$$. There are many ways/perspectives we could adopt to interpret that space/set. With the language of measure theory, we could assign numbers subsets of the space, hence measures, among which there's the Lebesgue measure, our usual perception of the Euclidean size of the underlying sapce. 

A personal note: I strongly felt that by diving deeper into math, we are sorting out definitions and our interpretations of the layered reality into its distinct fundamental constructs. Ok, let's go back to the topic. 

---

### 1.2.4] Equality of Measures
Perhaps not so surprisingly, two measures $$\mu$$ and $$\nu$$ are equal if they assign the same size to every measurable set. That is,

$$
\mu = \nu
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\mu(A)=\nu(A)
\text{ for every measurable set }A.
$$

This is an importantand subtle conceptual point:

> Measures are compared by what they do to sets, not points.

For example, if another measure $$\nu$$ satisfies

$$
\nu(A)=\lambda(A)
$$

for every region $$A$$, then

$$
\nu=\lambda.
$$

---

### 1.2.5] Flows of Vector Fields

Given a vector field

$$
\frac{dx_t}{dt} = V_t(x_t),
$$

the associated flow $$\phi_t$$ moves points through space. For a starting point $$x$$, $$\phi_t(x)$$ is the location of the trajectory at time $$t$$. The flow satisfies the given ODE/vector field:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\phi_t(x) =
V_t(\phi_t(x)),
\qquad
\phi_0(x)=x.
$$

Notice this is just the re-write of the usual ODE formulation together with the flow language. 

---
### 1.2.6] What Does a Flow Do to Volume?
Take a small blob of points $$A$$. Under the flow, it moves to $$\phi_t(A)$$. We now could ask: Does the blob keep the same volume? That means asking whether

$$
\lambda(\phi_t(A)) = \lambda(A).
$$

If this holds for every measurable set $$A$$, then the flow preserves Lebesgue measure. Notice that here we naturally employ the fact that $$\phi_t$$ maps from $$\mathbb{R}^n$$ to $$\mathbb{R}^n$$ so never leaving this space, consequently enabling the application of the given measure $$\lambda$$. 

---
### 1.2.7] Pushforward Measures

Suppose $$ \phi : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n $$ is a map. The pushforward measure $$\phi_\#\mu$$ is defined by

$$
(\phi_\#\mu)(A) = \mu(\phi^{-1}(A)).
$$

This definition might look mysterious at first, but the meaning is intuitive (actually in many branches of mathematics, there're many definitions based on the idea of "pullback"/"pushforward"). 

In a nutshell, the pushward measure is still a measure. The map acts on the space/set itself, which induces a measure, the pushforward measure. 

We could interpret it this way: Suppose particles move according to $$\phi$$. We then ask: “How much mass ends up inside a region $$A$$ after we apply this transformation $$\phi$$?” Well, to answer this, we first find all starting points that eventually land in $$A$$, and then measure how much mass originally lived there. The set of starting points is $$ \phi^{-1}(A)$$, so

$$
(\phi_\#\mu)(A) = \mu(\phi^{-1}(A)).
$$

Perhaps let me give me an easy example: stretching space. Consider the doubling map: $$\phi(x)=2x$$ on $$\mathbb{R}$$. Take the interval $$ A=[0,2]$$ and then $$ \phi^{-1}(A)=[0,1]$$. Therefore

$$
(\phi_\#\lambda)([0,2]) = \lambda([0,1]) = 1.
$$

But

$$
\lambda([0,2])=2.
$$

Hence

$$
\phi_\#\lambda \neq \lambda.
$$

The interpretation here is very intuitive: since the space is stretched by factor $$2$$, intervals become twice as long and then naturally the ordinary length is not preserved. 

Another simple positive example is translation. Consider $$ \phi(x)=x+1$$. Take $$ A=[5,8]$$ and then $$ \phi^{-1}(A)=[4,7]$$. Both intervals have length $$3$$, so

$$
(\phi_\#\lambda)(A) = \lambda(A).
$$

It's not hard to check that this holds for every set, therefore

$$
\phi_\#\lambda = \lambda.
$$

Translations preserve Lebesgue measure.

---
### 1.2.8] Measure-Preserving Map
With the above definitions of measure and pushforward, we naturally arrive at concept of a measure-preserving map. Specifically, a map $$\phi$$ is called measure-preserving, with respect to measure $$\lambda$$, if 

$$
\phi_\# \lambda = \lambda
$$


---
### 1.2.9] Divergence and Volume Change

With the model of a flow/vector field, characterization of measure-preservation could be made more succinct. Remember from calculus that divergence measures infinitesimal volume expansion. For a vector field (for notation simplicity, I omit the temporal variation of the vector field):  $$ V=(V_1,\dots,V_n)$$, the divergence is

$$
\operatorname{div}V = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial V_i}{\partial x_i}.
$$

Geometrically:
- positive divergence → nearby particles spread apart
- negative divergence → nearby particles compress
- zero divergence → no infinitesimal volume change

---
### 1.2.10] The Local Volume Law/Liouville's Theorem
Imagine a tiny moving cube carried by the flow and its volume $$V_t(x_t)$$ evolves according to $$ \frac{d}{dt}V_t(x_t)= (\operatorname{div}V_t)\,V_t(x_t)$$

From basic ODE thoeries we could immediately know that 

- $$\operatorname{div}V_t>0$$ → exponential expansion
- $$\operatorname{div}V_t<0$$ → contraction
- $$\operatorname{div}V_t=0$$ → constant volume

Now if we look back at 1.2.1 hopefully it should be very easy for us to grasp the essense of Liouville's theorem, which the above sits right at its geometric heart. 

Specifically, tet $$\phi_t$$ be the flow generated by $$V_t$$. The Jacobian determinant of the flow satisfies

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\det(D\phi_t(x_t)) = (\operatorname{div}V_t)(\phi_t(x_t))\, \det(D\phi_t(x_t)).
$$

This equation describes exactly how local volumes deform under the flow. If

$$
\operatorname{div}V_t(x_t)=0,
$$

then

$$
\det(D\phi_t(x_t))=1
$$

for all $$t$$. Therefore the flow preserves volume locally and globally.

Let me now state the theorem cleanly: 

> Let $$V_t$$ be a sufficiently smooth vector field with flow $$\phi_t$$. Then $$ \operatorname{div}V_t=0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad (\phi_t)_\#\lambda=\lambda$$.

Equivalently,

$$
\lambda(\phi_t(A)) = \lambda(A)
$$

for every measurable set $$A$$. In words, this means that a flow preserves ordinary volume exactly when its vector field is divergence-free.

---
### 1.2.11] A Few More Examples
Consider the following expanding flow: $$ V(x,y)=(x,y)$$. Then $$ \operatorname{div}V = 1+1 = 2 $$. After some simple calculation, the flow is $$\phi_t(x,y) = (e^t x,e^t y)$$. Lengths scale by $$e^t$$ in each direction, so areas scale by $$ e^{2t}$$ and thus the area is not preserved. 

On the other hand, consider a rotation:  $$V(x,y)=(-y,x)$$. This generates rotations and its divergence is $$ 0+0=0$$. The flow rotates regions without changing area and thus Lebesgue measure is preserved.

---

### 1.2.12] A More General/Nuanced Perspective

The equivalence

$$
\operatorname{div}V_t=0
\iff
(\phi_t)_\#\lambda=\lambda
$$

is specifically about Lebesgue measure. More generally, a flow preserves a measure $$\mu$$ if $$ (\phi_t)_\#\mu=\mu$$ (well, this is as what we stated above), but in this setting, the relevant condition becomes

$$
\operatorname{div}_\mu V_t = 0,
$$

where divergence is computed relative to the given measure (possibly not Lebesgue) $$\mu$$.

Also, notice that we don't have to refine ourselves to just the sample domain. A map $$\phi: X \rightarrow Y$$ is measure-preserving from $$(X,\mu)$$ to $$(Y,\nu)$$ if $$\phi_\# \mu = \nu$$ (this would later be the necessary condition/constraint for optimal transport). However, then we would not have a vector field to differentiablly characterize the flow changes. 

In the most general setting ($$\phi: X \rightarrow Y$$), a very practical way to check measure preservation is to run the following: For integrable functions $$f : Y \to \mathbb{R}$$:

$$
\int_Y f(y)d\nu(y) = \int_X f(\phi(x)) d\mu(x)
$$

This is often the most practical way to verify pushforward equality.

---

### 1.2.13] Measure, Point-wise Evaluation, and Density?
You may wonder why we use the language of measure, not density. Well, rigorously speaking, a measure is more general than density, which comes with a dozen necessary conditions/assumptions that are not always valid. This also relates to a perhaps common misunderstanding on the concept of equality of measure, where you might want to say $$\phi_\#\mu(x) = \nu(y)$$ for $$\phi_\#\mu = \nu$$ (I'm here using the more general notion, see 1.2.12]). This is simply just wrong because measures act on sets, not single points. 

However, if you are indeed persistent, asking when can we write something pointwise? Then we connect back to the density function: Only if both measures have densities with respect to a base measure (say Lebesgue).

Suppose $$d\mu(x) = p(x)dx, d\nu(y) = q(y)dy$$. meaning that the measures $$\mu, \nu$$ have densities $$p(x), q(y)$$, respectievly, respective to the Lebesgue measure. Let me be more explicit in case you are not familiar with this measure language of probability. When we write $$d\mu(x) = p(x)dx$$, this means the measure $$\mu$$ has density function $$p$$ relative to Lebesgue measure. Concretely, $$\mu(A) = \int_A p(x)dx$$ for every measurable set $$A$$. Similarly, $$d\nu(y) = q(y)dy$$ means $$\nu(B) = \int_B q(y)dy$$

Now, with the density specification, $$\phi_\#\mu = \nu$$ translates into a density transformation rule: If $$\phi$$ is invertible and smooth, then:

$$
q(y) = p(\phi^{-1}(y)) \det[D\phi^{-1}(y)]
$$

This is the closest thing to a “pointwise” relationship, but note that it’s between densities, not measures directly. Measures themselves are global objects, but once densities exist, we can express transport locally/pointwise through density transformation formulas. Conceptually, the measure $$\mu$$ is the true object
while $$p$$ is its coordinate representation relative to $$dx$$.

At this point, if it still does not click for you, this is what's called the change of variable formula from basic probability theory!

Before we leave this section, you might wonder when would we not use a Lebesgue measure? For a probability measure, if it's not relative to Lebesgue measure, what would its density be? What does that mean?

This actualy gets to the heart of what a measure really is. The key idea is that a density is always defined relative to some reference measure (ponder on what density really is). Lebesgue measure is just one possible reference measure — an extremely common one because it represents ordinary geometric volume, but many probability measures are naturally compared to something else.

However, not all probability distributions spread mass continuously across space. For examples, discrete distributions, measures on lower-dimensional manifolds, or singular/fractal measures. For these, Lebesgue measure is the wrong reference.

For example, for discrete probability measures: Suppose $$\mu = \sum p_i \delta_{x_i}$$, where $$\delta_{x_i}$$ is a Dirac mass. This assigns $$\mu(\{x_i\}) = p_i$$.​ We should see that this measure has no Lebesgue density. Why? Since single points have Lebesgue measure zero $$dx(\{x_i\}) = 0$$, yet $$\mu(\{x_i\}) > 0$$, no function $$p(x)$$ can satisfy $$\mu(A) = \int_A p(x)dx$$. 

Instead, the natural base measure is the counting measure: $$\#(A)$$ = number of elements in A. Then:

$$
d\mu(\{x_i\}) = p_i d\#(\{x_i\})
$$

and the “density” is simply the probability mass function.

Another counter-example is measures on manifolds. Suppose probability mass lies only on a circle in $$\mathbb{R}^2$$, say we use uniform distribution on the unit circle. This has no 2D Lebesgue density because the circle has area zero in $$\mathbb{R}^2$$. However, it does have density relative to arc-length measure on the circle. Consequently, $$d\mu = p(s) ds$$ where $$ds$$ is the intrinsic 1D measure along the curve. Without emphasizing on its importance, you might realize this is crucial in geometry and physics, where a surface measure is not compared to ambient volume measure.

For readers interested in figuring the precise relationships between measure and density, and what "with respect to base/Lebesgue measure" really encodes, please refer to some basic measure theory, and specificall look for Radon-Nikodym (A side note: it's also interesting to figure out the relation between this measure language of $$dx$$ vs the differential forms language of $$dx$$). 

Very briefly, the fully general statement is that if $$ \mu << \lambda$$, then there exists a density $$\frac{d\mu}{d\lambda}$$ such that 

$$
\mu(A) = \int_A \frac{d\mu}{d\lambda} d\lambda
$$

This derivative ​$$\frac{d\mu}{d\lambda}$$ is the Radon–Nikodym derivative. Consequently,“density” always means:

> how much $$\mu$$-mass per unit $$\lambda$$-mass

where $$\lambda$$ is our chosen reference/base measure.

One last reflection here, which I do think is very crucial to realize and internalize: Probability distributions/measures themselves are coordinate-free geometric objects. A “density function” only appears after choosing a reference measure. In some sense,  measures are intrinsic while densities are relative descriptions. This is philosophically very similar to:

> vectors vs coordinates
> tensors vs matrix representations
> manifolds vs charts

and this analogy becomes very important in differential geometry, stochastic calculus, optimal transport, and modern generative modeling.

Enough for now.

## 2.2] Examples

### 2.2.1] Deterministic vs. Product Coupling

Almost the opposite of product coupling and they are almost never the same, even in your setting where  

$$
\nu = \Phi_{\#}\mu .
$$

Let's go through the structure carefully, first of all deterministic coupling induced by the map. Let

$$
X \sim \mu, \qquad Y = \Phi(X).
$$

Then the joint distribution of $$(X,Y)$$ is

$$
\gamma = (\text{Id},\phi)_{\#}\mu
$$

Explicitly, for measurable sets $$A,B$$:

$$
\gamma(A \times B)
= \mu\big(\{x\in A : \phi(x)\in B\}\big).
$$

This coupling has marginals

- first marginal: $$\mu$$
- second marginal: $$\phi_{\#}\mu = \nu$$

so it is indeed a coupling between $$\mu$$ and $$\nu$$.

The key property for the above joint law is that the conditional distribution satisfies

$$
Y|X=x \sim \delta_{\phi(x)}(y).
$$

So **$$Y$$ is completely determined by $$X$$**. This is why it is called a **deterministic coupling**.

On the other hand, there always exist the produc coupling:

$$
\gamma_{\text{prod}} = \mu \otimes \nu.
$$

For measurable sets:

$$
\gamma_{\text{prod}}(A \times B)
= \mu(A)\nu(B).
$$

Its conditional distribution is

$$
Y|X=x \sim \nu.
$$

So here

- $$X$$ and $$Y$$ are **independent**.

The deterministic coupling and the product coupling are different. The deterministic coupling lives on the **graph of $$\phi$$**:

$$
\{(x,\phi(x))\}.
$$

whereas the product coupling spreads mass over the **entire product space**.

Well, this actually has some practical advantages for transport map instead of coupling (transport plan) in terms of parameter storage, and in fact quite often we will be able to find a transport map in optimal transport problem, which is indeed in most cases preferrable. We will come back to this later. 

### 2.2.2] An Example 

Let

$$
X \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1], \qquad \Phi(x)=x.
$$

Then

$$
\nu = \mu.
$$

For the deterministic coupling, the joint law is 

$$
(X,X)
$$

Support: $$ \{(x,x)\} $$, which is just the **diagonal line**.

On the other hand, for the product coupling, the joint law stays on

$$
(X,Y), \quad X,Y \text{ i.i.d. Uniform}[0,1]
$$

where the support is  $$ [0,1]^2 $$, which is the **whole square**.

These are clearly different distributions.

### 2.2.3] When Would They Coincide?

They coincide **only in degenerate cases**. For example if $$\nu$$ is a **Dirac measure**

$$
\nu = \delta_{y_0}
$$

and

$$
\Phi(x)=y_0.
$$

Then

- deterministic coupling: $(X,y_0)$
- product coupling: $(X,Y)$ with $Y=y_0$ a.s.

So they match in this specific case, but this is exceptional.

What I want to emphasize is that knowing that

$$
\nu = \Phi_{\#}\mu
$$

**does not restrict couplings to deterministic ones**.

It only tells us **one particular coupling exists**:

$$
(\text{Id},\Phi)_{\#}\mu .
$$

But the set of couplings

$$
\Pi(\mu,\nu)
$$

is huge — it includes

- deterministic couplings
- product coupling
- infinitely many others.

Later in **optimal transport**, we will observe that the deterministic coupling

$$
(\text{Id},\phi)_{\#}\mu
$$

is called a **transport map**, but most couplings are **transport plans** that cannot be written as maps.

In summary, if we indeed have a map $$Y=\Phi(X)$$, then we would naturally obtain a deterministic coupling 
$$
\gamma = (\text{Id},\phi)_{\#}\mu
$$

while in most cases it's different from product coupling, which always exists between two measures 

$$
\gamma = \mu\otimes\nu
$$

$$X$$ and $$Y$$ independent.

They are **almost never the same**.

## 4] A Broader Geometric Picture 

It's hard to conceal the deeper truth behind such differences and connections between deterministic vs product coupling. However, readers not familiar with optimal transport (I will cover that in the not too distance future) feel free to skip this and come back later. Specifically, here I would like to clarify how deterministic couplings sit inside the full coupling polytope and why optimal transport often selects them. The following applied to all couplings when $\nu = \Phi_{\#}\mu$

Suppose

$$
\nu = \Phi_{\#}\mu
$$

for some measurable map $$\Phi : X \to Y$$, or we could just say $$Y = \Phi(X), \qquad X \sim \mu
$$. 

This means 

$$
\gamma(X, Y) = \mu(X)\delta_{\Phi(X)}(Y)
$$

is **one possible coupling, or one transport plan** between $$\mu$$ and $$\nu$$.

However, from the above discussions we should already be intuitive and comfortable with the idea that the space of all couplings between $$\mu$$ and $$\nu$$ is much larger. Consequently, understanding this space geometrically helps clarify the role of deterministic transport maps.

---

## 4.1] The Space of All Couplings

The set of couplings is

$$
\Pi(\mu,\nu)
= \{\gamma \in \mathcal{P}(X \times Y) :
(\pi_X)_{\#}\gamma = \mu,\;
(\pi_Y)_{\#}\gamma = \nu
\}.
$$

So every coupling

- spreads mass over the product space $$X\times Y$$
- while preserving the two marginals.

Geometrically:

- $$\Pi(\mu,\nu)$$ is a **convex set** (I'll let readers to prove this claim)
- Mixtures of couplings remain couplings.

Indeed, if

$$
\gamma_1,\gamma_2 \in \Pi(\mu,\nu)
$$

then

$$
\gamma_t = t\gamma_1 + (1-t)\gamma_2, \,\, t \in [0, 1]
$$

is also a valid coupling (this is almost obvious).

So $\Pi(\mu,\nu)$ behaves like a **convex polytope (or convex body)** in the space of measures.

---

## 4.2] Deterministic Couplings = Graphs

The deterministic coupling induced by $\Phi$ is

$$
\gamma_{\Phi} = (\mathrm{Id},\Phi)_{\#}\mu.
$$

Its support is

$$
\text{supp}(\gamma_{\Phi})
= \{(x,\Phi(x))\}.
$$

which is the **graph of $$\Phi$$**.

So geometrically, all mass lies on a **lower-dimensional surface**, instead of filling the whole product space.

For example (the same as above), if

$$
X=Y=[0,1], \quad \Phi(x)=x
$$

then

- deterministic coupling → **diagonal line**
- product coupling → **full square**

---

## 4.3] Discrete Case: The Transportation Polytope

The geometry becomes very clear in the **finite case**.

Suppose

$$
\mu = (a_1,\dots,a_n), \qquad
\nu = (b_1,\dots,b_m).
$$

A coupling is a matrix

$$
\Gamma = (\gamma_{ij})
$$

satisfying

$$
\sum_j \gamma_{ij} = a_i
$$

$$
\sum_i \gamma_{ij} = b_j
$$

$$
\gamma_{ij} \ge 0
$$

This set forms a **transportation polytope**. Perhaps it's a little more intuitive to see that geometrically it's a convex polytope in $$\mathbb{R}^{nm}$$.

Consequently, for this problem the **vertices of this polytope** correspond to **deterministic transport plans**.

In particular

- extreme points have at most $$n+m-1$$ nonzero entries
- many correspond to **permutation-like assignments**.

These are the **most structured couplings**.

Another perspective is to interpret this as a __linear programming__ optimization problem, which I will elaborate more later when actually go into optimal transport!

---

## 4.4] Where the Deterministic Map Lives

When $$\nu = \Phi_{\#}\mu$$, the coupling $$\gamma_\Phi$$ lies inside $\Pi(\mu,\nu)$. More precisely, it sits on the **boundary** of this convex set. Why?

Because it concentrates mass on the **graph constraint**

$$
y=\Phi(x).
$$

This is a very **low-entropy configuration**. In contrast:

- product coupling spreads mass everywhere
- mixtures smear the distribution across the square.

---

## 4.5] Why Optimal Transport Selects Deterministic Maps

Optimal transport (Kantorovich problem) solves

$$
\min_{\gamma\in\Pi(\mu,\nu)}
\int c(x,y)\, d\gamma(x,y).
$$

But notice that this optimization problem is

> A **linear objective over a convex set**

Since

- $$\Pi(\mu,\nu)$$ is convex
- It's linear in $$\gamma$$
- deterministic plans correspond to extreme points

the optimizer often lands on a **deterministic transport map**.

---

## 4.6] Brenier's Theorem (quadratic cost)

If

- $$\mu$$ is absolutely continuous
- the cost is

$$
c(x,y)=|x-y|^2
$$

then the optimal coupling is

$$
\gamma = (\mathrm{Id},T)_{\#}\mu
$$

for a map

$$
T = \nabla \psi
$$

where $\psi$ is convex. Notice that the Brenier's Theorem is special not because it exposes that $$T$$ exists and is a gradient (which has been known to many even before Brenier for a long time), but that it's a gradient of a __convex__ function. Conseuqently, the optimal plan collapses onto a **deterministic graph**.

---

## 4.7] Entropy Perspective

Deterministic coupling corresponds to **minimum entropy**, while product coupling indicates **maximum entropy**. Later on we will discuss one practical alrogithm (Sinkhorn) for solving the optimal transport optimizaton problem, on a slightly regugalirzed form of this problem called Entropic Optimal Transport, which intentionally pushes solutions away from deterministic vertices toward the **interior of the polytope**.

---

## 4.8] Intuitive Summary

Key ideas:
- deterministic 
couplings sit on the **boundary/extreme points**
- mixtures fill the interior
- optimal transport prefers **extreme points**, which explains why **transport maps appear naturally**

Even if $$\nu=\Phi_{\#}\mu$$, the deterministic coupling is just **one corner of a huge convex set of possible couplings** — optimal transport often picks that corner because linear costs prefer **extreme points of the coupling polytope**.
