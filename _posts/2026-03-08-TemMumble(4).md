### 5.1] Ordinary Convexity in Probability Space
If we view probability measures as a linear space, the natural interpolation between two measures $$\mu$$ and $$\nu$$ is the **mixture interpolation**:

$$
\rho_t = (1-t)\mu + t\nu, \quad t \in [0,1].
$$

A functional $$F(\rho_t)$$ is convex in the usual sense if

$$
F\big((1-t)\mu + t\nu\big)
\le
(1-t)F(\mu) + tF(\nu).
$$

This is sometimes called **mixture convexity**. For example, negative entropy is convex under mixtures.

Specifically, recall that the **negative entropy** is

$$
F(\rho) = \int \rho(x) \log \rho(x)\, dx .
$$

(so the usual notion of entropy is $$H(\rho) = -F(\rho)$$). Then we have

$$
F((1-t)p + t q)
\le
(1-t)F(p) + tF(q).
$$

Substituting $$F(\rho)=\int \rho\log \rho$$,

$$
\int \rho_t \log \rho_t
\le
(1-t)\int p\log p + t\int q\log q .
$$

This is very intuitive, as entropy measures **uncertainty**, and mixing two distributions **increases uncertainty**, so entropy goes up:

$$
H((1-t)p + tq)
\ge
(1-t)H(p) + tH(q).
$$

Or, we could interpret this algebraically, since 

$$
\phi(x) = x \log x
$$

is **convex for $$x>0$$**.

Since

$$
F(\rho) = \int \phi(\rho(x)) dx,
$$

and integrals preserve convexity, $F$ becomes a **convex functional**.

Thus:

- **Entropy is (mixture) concave**
- **Negative entropy is (mixture) convex**

This property is fundamental because:

- It makes **KL divergence** a **Bregman divergence** of negative entropy.
- It ensures **variational problems involving entropy are convex**.
- It contrasts with **optimal transport geometry**, where entropy behaves differently (leading to **displacement convexity**).

There's also a **very deep connection** between

**negative entropy convexity $$\rightarrow$$ KL divergence convexity  $$\rightarrow$$ exponential families $$\rightarrow$$ mirror descent**

which is one of the most beautiful structures in information geometry. Later story.

### 5.2] Wasserstein Geometry: a Different Notion of Straight Line

Given the great properties above, however, many geometric functionals are **not convex under mixture interpolation**.

But hold on, you might ask, if mixture convexity holds for negative entropy, why bother studying other kinds of convexity?

This is an excellent and deep question. The short answer is: Mixture convexity lives in the wrong geometry for many physical and dynamical problems, whereas optimal transport reveals the correct geometry of probability space.

Importantly, mixture interpolation is not physical transport. Say we have 

$$
\rho_t = (1-t)p + tq
$$

this literally means flip a coin with heads up with probability $$t$$ and down with $$(1-t)$$. If it's Head, then sample from $$q$$ and sample from $$q$$ otherwise. Since we just randomly choose distribution $$p$$ or $$q$$, it does not move mass. In extreme case, if $$p = \delta_{-5}, q = \delta_{5}$$, then mixture interpolation gives us just two bumps, because both distributions coexist.

However, if we kind of expect the interpolation to be smearing/smoothing/averaging them together in some sense, which actually corresponds transport mass and just one bump moving from $$5$$ to $$-5$$. 

This is what Wasserstein geodesics produce.

In optimal transport, probability measures are not treated as a linear space but as a **Riemannian-like metric space** (Otto's theory, will elaborate in the following blogs)

$$
(\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d), W_2)
$$

where $W_2$ is the **2-Wasserstein distance**.

In this geometry, the natural notion of a **straight line** is a **Wasserstein geodesic**.

Suppose the optimal transport map from $$\mu$$ to $$\nu$$ is

$$
T(x).
$$

Then the geodesic interpolation is

$$
\rho_t = \big((1-t)I + tT\big)_{\#}\mu
$$

This describes particles moving along straight lines from $$x$$ to $$T(x)$$.

From the above we shall see that

- mixture interpolation = **blending distributions**
- optimal transport interpolation = **moving mass**

### 5.3] Displacement Convexity

A functional $$F(\rho)$$ is called **displacement convex** if along the Wasserstein geodesic $$\rho_t$$

$$
F(\rho_t)
\le
(1-t)F(\mu) + tF(\nu).
$$

---

### 5.4] Entropy is Displacement Convex

Consider the **differential entropy**

$$
H(\rho)= \int \rho(x)\log\rho(x)\,dx.
$$

(notice that here we're not using the negative sign (you could add if you want, then just switch convex to concave, not a problem at all)) A fundamental theorem by **Robert J. McCann (1997)** shows:

> The entropy functional is convex along Wasserstein geodesics.

So indeed

$$
t \mapsto H[\rho_t]
$$

is convex when $$\rho_t$$ is the optimal transport interpolation. This is called **McCann displacement convexity**.

This property implies:

* uniqueness of gradient flows
* stability of diffusion
* functional inequalities
* curvature bounds of spaces

Consequently, entropy behaves nicely in both convexities, but the displacement convexity describes real dynamics.

---

### 5.5] Why This Happens (Intuition)

The key reason is that **optimal transport preserves the structure of mass movement**.

Under optimal transport interpolation:

- the density evolves according to a **continuity equation**
- the **Jacobian of the transport map** controls density change.

Specifically,

$$
\rho_t(x) = \frac{\rho_0(y)}
{\det\big((1-t)I + tDT(y)\big)}.
$$

This determinant interacts with the $\log \rho$ term in entropy in a way that produces convexity.

This phenomenon is deeply related to:

- the **Brunn–Minkowski inequality**
- convexity of the **log-determinant function**

## 6] Many PDEs Follow Displacement Geometry

Displacement convexity is extremely important because it implies **gradient flows in Wasserstein space**. Indeed, apart from entropy, many other important equations evolve along Wasserstein geometry, not mixture geometry. For example, 

* Fokker–Planck equation:

$$
\partial_t \rho = \nabla \cdot (\rho \nabla V) + \Delta \rho
$$

Jordan–Kinderlehrer–Otto (1998) showed:

> This PDE is gradient descent of entropy in Wasserstein space.

Specifically,

$$
\partial_t \rho
= \nabla \cdot (\rho \nabla \log \rho) = \Delta \rho
$$

So entropy is not just concave along mixtures. It behaves as a convex energy along OT geodesics, and thus heat equation can be interpreted as the **gradient flow of entropy in Wasserstein space**.

---

## 7] Why This was a Big Discovery

Before optimal transport, probability space was treated as a linear space. Optimal transport revealed it actually has a Riemannian-like geometry. In that geometry:

* geodesics = mass transport
* entropy = convex energy
* diffusion = gradient flow

This unified PDE, probability, geometry, and statistical physics.
