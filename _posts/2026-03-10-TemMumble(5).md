#### 2.2.2] Relation with Legendre Transform


It’s useful to understand the **Legendre transform** from several viewpoints which appear in **convex analysis**, **symplectic/differential geometry**, and **optimal transport duality**. They are mathematically equivalent but emphasize different structures.

###### 2.2.2.1] Convex analysis viewpoint (Fenchel–Legendre transform)

Let $$f : \mathbb{R}^n \to (-\infty,\infty]$$.

The **Legendre transform** (or convex conjugate) is

$$
f^*(p) = \sup_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} \left( p \cdot x - f(x) \right)
$$

This means that for each vector $$p$$, we measure how well the linear function

$$
x \mapsto p \cdot x
$$

can dominate $$f(x)$$.

Equivalently,

$$
f(x) \ge p \cdot x - f^*(p)
$$

for all $$x$$.

So $$f^*(p)$$ describes the **supporting hyperplane with slope $$p$$**. In other words, $$f^*(p)$$ is the intercept of the function $$p \cdot x$$. 

Indeed, the affine function

$$
\ell_p(x) = p \cdot x - f^*(p)
$$

satisfies

$$
\ell_p(x) \le f(x)
$$

for all $$x$$.

On the other hand, $$f$$ can be thus reconstructed as

$$
f(x) = \sup_p \big( p\cdot x - f^*(p) \big)
$$

This is the **biconjugate theorem**:

$$
f^{**} = f
$$

for convex lower semicontinuous $$f$$.

If $$f$$ is smooth and strictly convex,

$$
p = \nabla f(x)
$$

and

$$
x = \nabla f^*(p).
$$

Thus

$$
\nabla f : x \mapsto p
$$

is inverted by

$$
\nabla f^*.
$$

This is extremely important in **information geometry** and **mirror descent**.

---

###### 2.2.2.2] Differential Geometry Viewpoint

Another way to interpret Legendre transform is to consider it as a **change of coordinates in the cotangent bundle**.

Consider the graph

$$
(x, \nabla f(x))
$$

inside

$$
\mathbb{R}^n_x \times \mathbb{R}^n_p.
$$

This is a submanifold of the **cotangent bundle**

$$
T^*\mathbb{R}^n.
$$

Define the function

$$
F(x,p) = p\cdot x - f(x).
$$

The Legendre transform arises when we **maximize over \(x\)**.

Consequently, the geometric picture is the following:  

The graph of $$df$$

$$
p = \nabla f(x)
$$

is a **Lagrangian submanifold** in the symplectic manifold

$$
(T^*\mathbb{R}^n, \omega = dp \wedge dx).
$$

The Legendre transform simply reparametrizes this same Lagrangian manifold using the variable $$p$$ instead of $$x$$.

So:

- $$f(x)$$ describes the manifold using **x-coordinates**
- $$f^*(p)$$ describes the same manifold using **p-coordinates**

This viewpoint is fundamental in:

- classical mechanics
- symplectic geometry
- Hamiltonian/Lagrangian duality.

---

##### 2.2.2.3] Classical Mechanics Example

In Lagrangian mechanics

$$
L(q,v)
$$

define momentum

$$
p = \frac{\partial L}{\partial v}.
$$

Then the **Hamiltonian** is

$$
H(q,p) = \sup_v \left( p\cdot v - L(q,v) \right).
$$

This is exactly a **Legendre transform in the velocity variable**.

---

##### 2.2.2.4] Optimal Transport Viewpoint

Now we arrive at why this matters for optimal transport. In **Kantorovich duality**, we define the **c-transform**: Given a cost function $$c(x,y)$$, we define 

$$
\phi^c(y) = \inf_x \left(c(x,y) - \phi(x)\right).
$$

Comparing this with Legendre transform:

$$
f^*(p) = \sup_x (p\cdot x - f(x)).
$$

This is a **c-transform with cost**

$$
c(x,p) = -x\cdot p.
$$

Thus

**Legendre transform = special case of c-transform with linear cost.**. Specifically, for quadratic cost

$$
c(x,y) = \frac{|x-y|^2}{2}
$$

the c-transform becomes

$$
\phi^c(y) = \inf_x \left(\frac{|x-y|^2}{2} - \phi(x)\right).
$$

Then optimal transport potentials satisfy

$$
\phi^{cc} = \phi.
$$

This mirrors the property

$$
f^{**} = f
$$

in convex analysis.

Consequently, there's also a deep connection between the gradient map $$\nabla f$$ and the optimal transport map (see below!)

You might wonder then why would Legendre transform appear in OT? Well, if we dissect the quadratic cost

$$
c(x,y)=\frac{|x-y|^2}{2}
$$

as 

$$
\frac{|x-y|^2}{2} = \frac{|x|^2}{2} + \frac{|y|^2}{2} - x\cdot y.
$$

It's not hard to see that the OT dual problem essentially contains a **hidden Legendre transform**.

That is why Brenier's theorem eventually gives the map

$$
T(x) = \nabla \psi(x)
$$

for a convex potential $$\psi$$.

---

##### 2.2.2.5] Intuition That Unifies Everything
The Legendre transform switches between two equivalent descriptions of the same object: while the variable $$(x, f(x))$$ represent the function values, $$(p, f^*(p))$$ indicate the supporting hyperplanes. 

In optimal transport, the same idea becomes that $$(x, \phi(x))$$ represent the potentials, whereas $$(y, \phi^c(y))$$ indicate the cost-adapted hyerplanes --- the **c-transform is simply the Legendre transform adapted to the geometry induced by the transport cost**.

This **single most important example** $$ c(x,y)=\frac{|x-y|^2}{2} $$ reveals why **optimal maps are gradients of convex functions**.

Actually the reason Brenier's Theorem is famous is not that the transport map exists and could be written as a gradient, but that it's a gradient of a __convex__ function. 

Also notice that in Brenier's theorem we only restrict on properties of $$\mu$$, the source measure, not $$\nu$$ the target measure, which echoes the exception of flowing from a Dirac not towards a Dirac!
