#### 4.3] Another Interpretation of Conditional Expectation

The statement you’re referring to is the **Hilbert space interpretation of conditional expectation**. The idea is that conditional expectation is the **best \(L^2\) approximation (least-squares projection)** of a random variable onto the space of functions measurable with respect to the conditioning σ-algebra.

I’ll explain the pieces carefully.

---

# 1. The \(L^2\) space of random variables

Consider a probability space \((\Omega,\mathcal F,P)\).

Define

$$
L^2(\Omega) = \{X : \mathbb{E}[X^2] < \infty \}.
$$

This is a **Hilbert space** with inner product

$$
\langle X,Y \rangle = \mathbb{E}[XY].
$$

The norm is

$$
\|X\|_2 = \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]}.
$$

This is exactly the **mean-square norm**.

---

# 2. The subspace of measurable functions

Let \(\mathcal G \subseteq \mathcal F\) be a σ-algebra.

Define

$$
L^2(\mathcal G)
$$

as the set of all **square-integrable random variables measurable with respect to \(\mathcal G\)**.

This is a **closed linear subspace** of \(L^2(\Omega)\).

So geometrically:

- \(L^2(\Omega)\) = a Hilbert space  
- \(L^2(\mathcal G)\) = a subspace

---

# 3. Conditional expectation as orthogonal projection

For any \(X \in L^2(\Omega)\), the conditional expectation

$$
\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal G]
$$

is **the orthogonal projection of \(X\) onto \(L^2(\mathcal G)\)**.

Meaning:

$$
\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G] =
\operatorname*{argmin}_{Y \in L^2(\mathcal G)} \mathbb{E}[(X-Y)^2]
$$

So it **minimizes the \(L^2\) error**.

---

# 4. Meaning of “\(L^2\) normalization error”

The phrase you saw likely meant:

> Conditional expectation minimizes the **\(L^2\) error** (mean-square error) among all \(\mathcal G\)-measurable functions.

Explicitly,

$$
\mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G])^2]
=
\min_{Y \in L^2(\mathcal G)} \mathbb{E}[(X-Y)^2]
$$

So if you want to approximate \(X\) using only information in \(\mathcal G\), the **best approximation in mean-square sense** is the conditional expectation.

---

# 5. Orthogonality condition

The projection property implies

$$
\mathbb{E}\big[(X-\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G])Y\big] = 0
\quad
\forall Y \in L^2(\mathcal G)
$$

So the **error is orthogonal to the subspace**.

This is the Hilbert-space analogue of least squares regression.

---

# 6. Simple example

Suppose

- \(X\) = tomorrow's temperature
- \(\mathcal G\) = today's weather information

Then

$$
\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G]
$$

is the **best mean-square prediction** of tomorrow’s temperature using today’s information.

No other \(\mathcal G\)-measurable predictor has smaller

$$
\mathbb{E}[(X-Y)^2]
$$

---

# 7. Geometric picture

Think of:

- \(X\) = a vector in Hilbert space  
- \(L^2(\mathcal G)\) = a subspace

Then

$$
\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G]
$$

is the **perpendicular projection of \(X\)** onto that subspace.

And

$$
X = \mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G] + (X-\mathbb{E}[X|\mathcal G])
$$

with the two parts orthogonal.

---

# 8. Why this interpretation is powerful

This viewpoint connects probability with:

- **Hilbert space theory**
- **least squares regression**
- **martingales**
- **signal filtering (Kalman filters)**

For example, **linear regression** is just a finite-dimensional case of this projection principle.

---

✅ **In one sentence**

Conditional expectation is the **orthogonal projection of a random variable onto the subspace of functions measurable with respect to the conditioning σ-algebra**, which means it **minimizes the \(L^2\) (mean-square) approximation error**.

## 9] Discussion

### 9.1] Why Learn an ODE instead of a one-step map?
This is a key question lying at the heart of modern generative modeling. The discussions below could equally be applied to diffusion models (just swap ODE into SDE, but fundamentally the same). 

Well, the short answer is that it is **too hard, too unstable, and too unconstrained**.  Instead, we learn a *velocity field* and integrate it, turning the problem into something **locally simple, globally expressive, and well-posed**.

---

#### 9.1.1] The “One-Step Map” Perspective

True, we might want to directly learn a map:
$$
T: x_0 \mapsto x_1
$$

Notice that this in somese corresponds to the **Monge transport problem**:

$$
T_\# p_0 = p_1
$$

In theory, such a map exists (e.g. the Brenier map under certain conditions).

---

#### 9.1.2] Problem 1: A Global, Highly Constrained Object

Notice that a valid transport map must satisfy the following:
- Push forward the *entire distribution*
- Preserve mass (no duplication or collapse)
- Satisfy structural constraints (e.g. monotonicity / convexity)

Thus we have a lot of challenges:
- We only observe samples, not densities
- Local errors lead to global inconsistencies
- No straightforward supervision signal

This means that we are trying to learn a **global diffeomorphism in one shot**. 

---

#### 9.1.3] Problem 2: Non-Identifiability

There are infinitely many maps $$T$$ such that:
$$
T_\# p_0 = p_1
$$

So thhe problem is **ill-posed**: The model can learn arbitrary, unstable mappings. On the contrary, flow methods fix this by choosing a **specific path** between distributions (notice that we define the conditional probability flow).

---

#### 9.1.4] Key Idea: Reduce Global to Local
This brings us to the core of such interpretation: Instead of learning:
$$
T(x)
$$

We learn a **time-dependent vector field**:
$$
v_t(\Phi(x_t))
$$

and define:
$$
\frac{d\Phi(x_t)}{dt} = v_t(\Phi(x_t))
$$

Then:
$$
T = \Phi_{0 \to 1}
$$

This is much easier because we only need to learn:

> How points move locally at each time

instead of:

> Where points end up globally

Specifically, in flow matching or diffusion, we construct conditional paths $$p(x_t \mid x_0, x_1)$$

Training becomes:
$$
\min_{\theta, z} \mathbb{E} \|v^\theta_t(x_t) - v_t^\star(x_t \mid z)\|^2
$$

This is loocal, pointwise, and stable (this is just a regression problem, how could it be simpler than this?)

For One-step map, it directly maps from noise to data, where small errors could potentially lead to catastrophic failure, whereas for flow models, the gradual transformation ensures that errors are small and correctable. 

Intuitively, this is consistent with our expectation that a composition of small maps is stable, and an one big map tends to be fragile. 

---

#### 9.1.5] Geometry: Flows Guarantee Consistency

Another perspective to look at this is that an ODE:

$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = v_t(x)
$$

Automatically gives:
- Continuous distribution evolution
- Diffeomorphic transformations (under conditions)

This ensures that there's no mass teleportation, instead juust smooth transport. On the other hand, a one-step neural network provides none of these guarantees.

---

#### 9.1.6] Connection to Optimal Transport

Instead of:
$$
\min_T \text{transport cost}
$$

We could solve:
$$
\min_{v_t} \int_0^1 \|v_t\|^2 dt
$$

This is the **Benamou–Brenier formulation**:
- Transport via velocity fields over time
- Much easier optimization

---

#### 9.1.7] Another Perspective

Learning a one-step map is like solving a:

- **Boundary value problem**

Learning a flow is like solving an:

- **Initial value problem**

Boundary value problems is hard, while initial value problems is more stable and incremental
