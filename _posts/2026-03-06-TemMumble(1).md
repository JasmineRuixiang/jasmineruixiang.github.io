### 0] High Dimensional Sampling
Sampling from a complex, arbitrary, high-dimensional distribution—like trying to find a needle in a haystack where the haystack is the size of the solar system—is one of the most significant hurdles in modern statistics and machine learning. When we say **"arbitrary,"** we mean the distribution doesn't follow a nice, predictable shape like a Gaussian (Bell Curve). When we say **"high-dimensional,"** we mean we are dealing with hundreds, thousands, or even millions of variables simultaneously.

---

#### 0.1] The Curse of Dimensionality
As the number of dimensions ($$d$$) increases, the "volume" of the space grows exponentially. If we want to maintain the same density of sample points in 100-dimensional space as we have in 2-dimensional space, we would need more samples than there are atoms in the observable universe.

* **Concentration of Measure:** In high dimensions, most of the "mass" of a distribution lives in a very thin "shell" or manifold far away from the mode (the peak) (check [this blogs](https://sander.ai/2020/09/01/typicality.html) and [this blog](https://www.inference.vc/high-dimensional-gaussian-distributions-are-soap-bubble/)). 
* **The Search Problem:** Most of the space is "empty" (near-zero probability). A random sampler will spend almost all its time exploring regions that contribute nothing to the overall understanding of the distribution.

---

#### 0.2] The Normalization Constant (The Partition Function)
Most complex distributions are defined by a function $$f(x)$$, but to make it a true probability distribution, it must integrate to 1. This requires dividing by a normalization constant $$Z$$:

$$P(x) = \frac{f(x)}{Z} \text{ where } Z = \int f(x) dx$$

In high dimensions, calculating this integral is computationally intractable. While algorithms like **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)** allow us to sample without knowing $$Z$$, they face significant efficiency bottlenecks.

(> **Note:** Modern techniques like **Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC)** attempt to solve these issues by using the gradient of the distribution to "glide" along the geometry, much like a puck sliding on a frictionless surface). 

---

#### 0.3] Multimodality and "Islands"
A complex distribution often has multiple "modes" (peaks) separated by vast valleys of low probability.

* **Trapping:** A sampler might get stuck in one peak, unaware that a much larger peak exists elsewhere. 
* **Mixing Time:** The time it takes for a sampler to "forget" its starting position and move between these islands grows exponentially with the height and width of the barriers between them.



---

#### 0.4] Anisotropy and Local Geometry
In high dimensions, the distribution might be very "stretched" in some directions and "compressed" in others. 

* **Step Size Dilemma:** If your sampler takes steps that are too large, it overshoots the narrow regions and gets rejected. If the steps are too small, it takes forever to explore the long, stretched dimensions.
* **Correlation:** When variables are highly correlated, the "path" the sampler must follow is a narrow, winding ridge in a massive space.

### 1] Probability Flows
Flows of probability measures underly a variety of techniques and theories, ranging from generative modeling like Diffusion and Flow based models, or more classical domains of math like optimal transport. Many seemingly intuitive formulations carry with themselves deep fundamental explanations. This blog will start from a very basic survey of options of changing, the different probability flows. But before that, to have an intuitive feeling of probability flows embedded in our mind, let's start with a glimpse of one of our ultimate problems to solve, the continuous Kantarovich transport problem:

$$
\inf_{\pi \in \Pi(X, Y)} \int c(x, y)d\pi(x, y)
$$

where $$c: \mathbb{R}^d \times \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$$ is a selected cost function, $$X, Y \in \mathbb{R}^d$$ random variables (notice that here this is actually not a rigorous notation for rv) from proability measures $$\mu, \nu$$ respectively, and $$\Pi(X, Y)$$ is the space of couplings between $$\mu$$ and $$\nu$$. 

Let's say we simplify the above by further requiring that $$\mu = \nu$$ and let's say that $$X$$ and $$Y$$ are independent. If this is the case, then what is the best coupling? 

Very obviously, the answer is that it will be an identity coupling, basically not to move the probability measures at all. On the other hand, the free option, product coupling, will result in 

$$
E(|X - Y|^2) = Var(X)
$$

which is positive. Consequently, althought we always have methods to move measures from one to the other, among these various selection to find out the "optimal" one is never a trivial question. 

For all the blogs in this series, I will observe the convention that $$\mu$$ is the source measure, $$\nu$$ is the target measure, and I'll not repeat this specification unless otherwise it changes. 

Our basic question framework goes as the following: Given $$\mu, \nu$$, we want to find out $${\rho}_{t \in [0, 1]}$$ such that $$\rho_0 = \mu, \rho_1 = \nu$$.

### 2] Continuity Equation
The `Continuity Equation` is the mathematical expression of a "no-leak" policy. In fluid dynamics, it represents the conservation of mass; in probability theory and optimal transport, it represents the **conservation of total probability mass** (ensuring the integral remains $1$ as the distribution evolves; or, the measure is preserved).

#### 2.1] Derivation
Imagine a fixed region of space (a "control volume") $V$ with a boundary surface $S$. If the amount of "stuff" (mass, probability, or charge) inside $V$ changes over time, it **must** be because that stuff flowed across the boundary $S$. 

We define:
* $$\rho_t(x)$$: The density at point $x$ and time $t$.
* $$\mathbf{v}_t(x)$$: The velocity field (or vector field) pushing the density.

Then, the total amount of "stuff" $$M$$ inside the volume $$V$$ is given by the volume integral:

$$M = \int_V \rho_t(x) dV$$

The rate of change of this mass over time is $$\frac{dM}{dt}$$. According to the conservation principle:

> **Rate of change inside $V$ = $-$(Net flux passing out through boundary $S$)**

The flux through a tiny element of the surface $$dS$$ is $$\rho \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$$, where $$\mathbf{n}$$ is the outward-pointing unit normal vector. Thus:

$$\frac{d}{dt} \int_V \rho_t dV = -\oint_S (\rho \mathbf{v}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS$$

To turn this into a purely local (differential) equation, we need to convert the surface integral into a volume integral. We use the **Divergence Theorem** (also known as Gauss's Theorem):

$$\oint_S (\rho \mathbf{v}) \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \int_V \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) dV$$

Substituting this back into our conservation equation:

$$\int_V \frac{\partial \rho_t}{\partial t} dV = -\int_V \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) dV$$

Since this equality must hold true for **any** arbitrary volume $V$, the integrands themselves must be equal at every point in space. By moving everything to one side, we arrive at the **Continuity Equation**:

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) = 0$$

---

#### 2.2] Interpretation of Terms
* **$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}$**: The local rate of change of density at a fixed point.
* **$\nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v})$**: The **divergence** of the flux. If this is positive, it means the flow is "spreading out" from that point, leading to a decrease in local density.

---

#### 2.3] Connection to Optimal Transport & ML
In Optimal Transport and Generative Modeling (like **Flow-matching** or **Diffusion models**), we use this to describe how a simple base distribution $\rho_0$ deforms into a complex data distribution $\rho_1$.

Instead of moving "particles" randomly, we solve for a **velocity field** $\mathbf{v}_t$ that transports the probability mass efficiently. This is the core of the **Benamou-Brenier** formulation:

$$\min_{\rho, \mathbf{v}} \int_0^1 \int_{\mathbb{R}^d} \rho(x, t) \|\mathbf{v}(x, t)\|^2 dx dt$$
subject to
$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) = 0$$

This constraint ensures that as we "morph" the distribution, we aren't creating or destroying probability—we are simply moving it along the most energy-efficient paths.
